A while ago I read [The Terraformers by Annalee Newitz](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/60784471-the-terraformers). It's a good Sci-fi book!

## What's it about?

You initially follow Destry, who's working to keep the environment in balance on a new planet ... for a real estate company. They find some unexpected inhabitants, and the rest of the book follows different members of the team as they deal with the fallout over the years.

## Spoiler free review

The best bit about the book is definitely the world building. It's very imaginative, yet slowly places its blocks. Near the second half you will be following along with terms that would sound ridiculous to an outsider -- but in a good way!

There are some time skips in the story, but it does show the development over a long time. It's called "The Terraformers", so it'll be weird if the place transformed overnight. There are also good ties between the parts, and when I re-read it I saw the hints / foreshadowing better.

There are some romantic subplots adjacent to the storylines. Sometimes the scenes get a bit queasy for me, but I think not much is lost if you skim the pages.

Speaking of the characters, they're good! (I couldn't find a better adjective -- that's why I'm not an author.) They feel at least two-dimensional, and have interests and opinions in line / outside of their thoughts. 
There's also good non-binary representation in both "robotic" and "organic" characters, and it doesn't hit you on the head with it. (Mostly "yeah they exist in the world and people don't complain about it.")

Finally, this wouldn't be a good sci-fi book without some nice Easter eggs and being relatable.

Again, the world building is bloody amazing. It slowly eases you into the idea of expanding the definition of "people": first just humans, then Archea (humanoid), and ending with the typical robot / "animal". By part 3 the main characters are a train and a cat. I also felt natural and nodding along with the extension. If the book started with this I'll probably be like "yeah this is weird, I'm just going to find another book."

Splitting the whole book into 3 main stories wasn't a bad choice. Each part showcases how far Sask-E has evolved, and the maps at the start of each part are a good indicator as well. Each story has clear arcs:

1. Destry as someone who's initially just doing her job to literally bargaining her future away for Spider City to exist
2. Sulfur to initially hating Meesha to liking him and falling in love (okay I lied a bit about the romantic subplot not tying into main storyline) and getting him onboard to making the trains [autonomous](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/28209634-autonomous).
3. Scrubjay from initially just liking to carry passengers around to realising the horrors of people outside of Spider City experience.

The arcs and characters also don't overstay their welcome: the first part ends after the contract is signed, and the second part ends after they decided to grow trains.

The callbacks / foreshadowing are nice and help me link back to existing parts: The Tongue Forks, naked mole rats, worms, and more.

The romance / sex scene subplots are ... alright? I think the first and third parts do it right with artistic description -- and yes, I even gave a pass to the naked people dancing along poles. But describing the fabric put on one's body for pleasure? The author is definitely being sex positive, but maybe a bit much for me.

The bridges between parts are done much better than what I just did. They are also good breaks to show what Verdance / Emerald are thinking, and introduce or flesh out characters. They are written as reactions to what our main cast were doing over the last section, so don't feel out of place.

The Easter eggs were good choices too: the travelling ~~salesman~~ herbivore problem, and Sunbuilders as a Civilisation-like game (I haven't played civ before, don't @ me).
